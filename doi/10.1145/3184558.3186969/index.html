I Read but Don't Agree: Privacy Policy Benchmarking using Machine Learning and the EU GDPR

WWW '18: Proceedings of The Web Conference 2018, Lyon, France, April 2018

With the continuing growth of the Internet landscape, users share large amount of personal, sometimes, privacy sensitive data. When doing so, often, users have little or no clear knowledge about what service providers do with the trails of personal data they leave on the Internet. While regulations impose rather strict requirements that service providers should abide by, the defacto approach seems to be communicating data processing practices through privacy policies. However, privacy policies are long and complex for users to read and understand, thus failing their mere objective of informing users about the promised data processing behaviors of service providers. To address this pertinent issue, we propose a machine learning based approach to summarize the rather long privacy policy into short and condensed notes following a risk-based approach and using the European Union (EU) General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) aspects as assessment criteria. The results are promising and indicate that our tool can summarize lengthy privacy policies in a short period of time, thus supporting users to take informed decisions regarding their information disclosure behaviors.

Keywords: privacy; privacy policy; data protection regulation

1 Introduction

Nowadays, The number of websites in the world wide web has reached over a billion [9]. These websites are accessed by almost half of the world's population on daily basis. This has resulted in Internet services fundamentally changing the way we interact with each other. These on-line interactions also result in a huge amount of personal digital trails, which is being collected and processed by service providers, quite often with users being unaware of what data is being collected for what purpose. In the end, users are left without control of their own data, creating a huge information asymmetry in contrast to the service providers who have full access. To address this issue, regulatory bodies have devised compliance guidelines and responsibilities for service providers that aim to preserve user's informational self-determination rights. As such, privacy policies have emerged to be the de facto transparency boards that service providers use to communicate their information processing practices.

While privacy policies also function as legal agreements between service providers and their users [12], a number of studies have shown that these documents suffer from bad readability indexes [1, 8, 10]. In effect, users rarely read these documents, mainly due to their complexity, use of technical jargons and excessive length. Reading privacy policies is not only cumbersome, but it also consumes a lot of time that would cause tremendous harm to the economy. McDonald and Cranor [6] showed in 2008 that if a user was to read the privacy policy of every service she visits on the Internet, she would, on average, need 244 hours annually which is slightly more than half of the average time a user would spend on the Internet by then. This becomes even more complicated and time consuming in today's Internet services infrastructure, where the number of on-line services has more than doubled since then and data is also shared with ”third parties” which have their own different privacy policies [3]. E.g., an excerpt from CNN's 1 rather long privacy policy states: ”Some of our Sites contain links to or integrations with other sites such as Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn, etc., whose information practices may be different than ours. Visitors should consult the other sites’ privacy notices as we have no control over information that is submitted to, or collected by, these third parties.”

While the Internet continues to grow, service providers and other data processors are continuously being challenged to abide by data protection regulations. One such regulation is the newly adopted European Union (EU) General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) [4]. The GDPR takes, among other things, transparency and user consent as core components wherein the responsibilities of the data processor and the rights of the user are specified. Especially, article 13 emphasizes on the way information that must be provided to the data subject at the time their personal data is collected has to be presented. Similarly, article 12 states that information must be provided in ”a concise, transparent, intelligible and easily accessible form, using clear and plain language, in particular for any information addressed specifically to a child”. Our motivation in this work is, thus, mainly influenced by the GDPR and newly emerging techniques of machine learning. We aim to support Internet users by simplifying privacy policies’ readability. We take the requirements stated in the GDPR as a basis and provide a privacy policy benchmarking tool that considers privacy aspects and through the use of risk indicators we draw users’ attention to the most relevant parts of the privacy policy. To achieve this, we introduce a categorization of data based on the interpretation of the GDPR and propose a risk based approach to privacy policy benchmarking. We use supervised machine learning techniques to classify the lengthy privacy policies and employ a three scale risk based categorization. As a result we simplify the interpretation of the privacy policy and draw users attention to the proposed risk indicators.

2 Methodology and Implementation

2.1 Privacy Aspect and Risk Assessment Criteria

The privacy aspects of the EU GDPR needed to be interpreted before they could be used as assessment criteria for privacy policies. While the EU GDPR is the legal boundary of privacy policies, it does not reflect the wording and formulation that is commonly used. Research works such as Costante et al. [2] have defined their privacy aspects mainly based on regulations, however, others used empirical techniques like quantitative user studies [14] or qualitative expert interviews [13]. For the definition of classification categories, we adapted the privacy aspects of the EU GDPR based on combinations of definitions of the aspects (cf. Figure 1). Article 9 is specifically used to asses how service providers promise to treat specially privacy sensitive information types.

Once the main categories were defined, a set of application privacy policies were analyzed by a group of privacy experts, as a result, three risk levels for each of the categories were determined.

2.2 Privacy Policy Corpus

Supervised machine learning techniques take as an input a training data set that must be manually labelled beforehand, so that afterwards the algorithm can generalize and classify further input data of similar nature. Thus, in order to build up the ’privacy corpus’, 45 privacy policies were collected taking into consideration the 45 most accessed websites in Europe according to the results provided by Alexa.com.

In order to perform the manual labeling task, 35 participants were recruited: 68,57% male and 31.43% female, all of them with at least bachelor degree, and ages ranging between 21 and 29 years old. They were provided with a description of the task, detailed instructions and explanations for each of the eleven categories and their a potential risk level (including descriptive examples). Finally, to facilitate the labeling task, they were provided with an annotation form with drop-downs and text passages describing the categories. On average, each participant extracted 1266 text passages from a single privacy policy, resulting on 443 text passages assigned to a privacy category and classified with a risk level.

2.3 Preprocessing

PrivacyGuide applies stop words removal, stemming, and tokenization as preprocessing measures before extracting the features using TF-IDF.

2.4 Classification

The choice of a perfect Machine Learning algorithm for a specific task remains unanswered by the research community. Related studies have therefore focused on creating algorithm portfolios from which they choose the best performing ones [5]. Since, the problem space is intuitively a classification one, we defined a multi-class approach (i.e., eleven privacy aspects and three associated risks levels for each of the categories), and four well known classifiers namely: Naive Bayes, Support Vector Machine (SVM), Decision Tree (DT), and Random Forest that fulfill this criteria were implemented (cf. Figure 3). The source code can also be found in a Github repository2. Certain privacy sentences may also fall into more than one category, thus we also implemented the Binary Relevance multi-label classification technique. We then compared the results using established evaluation metrics, i.e., precision, recall and finally f-measure as weighted harmonic average of both.

2.5 Privacy Policy Evaluation Presentation

Two information levels have been considered: a high-level information (by default), comprised of indicators representing each privacy aspect (category) and the resulting evaluation (risk level); and, a detailed information level, providing the privacy aspect description, meaning of the risk level, and finally, the privacy policy fragment associated to the privacy aspects and its evaluation.

3 Results and Summary

Before building the final model, we ran experiments on the four algorithms (cf. Section 2.4). While Naive Bayes and SVM seem to be robust in the available training and testing data, Decision Tree and Random Forest showed low precision. This could be due to both the size of the training data and presence of some ”noise” in it.

Naive Bayes and SVM have relatively similar performance. However, SVM takes more time compared to Naive Bayes, thus, the final model and the results indicated in Table 1 and Figure 2 (b) are based on the Naive Bayes classifier. Furthermore, Naive Bayes performed even better in the risk classification phase resulting in average f-measure of 90% across the aspects.

We also observe that, in contrast to the reports in [6] as to how long it would take an average user to finish reading a privacy policy, PrivacyGuide summarizes the privacy policies in a short period of time, helping users save a significant amount of time. Nevertheless, the time that is needed for loading a privacy policy can be affected by a variety of external reasons like: the websites’ response time, the Internet connection, the bandwidth, the amount of content that is displayed on the same page and many more. Further observations show that despite the requirements in the GDPR for data breach notifications, our assessments showed that none of the Internet services we analyzed have plans for privacy breach notification (cf. Table 1 data breach notification results).

In summary, the benefits of the PrivacyGuide results are two fold: 1) supporting users in assessing or interpreting privacy policies; 2) providing actionable insights for service providers with respect to their privacy policies. Moreover, it can also trigger discussions among data protection officers, lawyers, privacy lobbyists, and other interested entities.

4 Demonstration, Limitations and Future Works

Demonstration:. During the demo session, we will showcase how the tool can extract privacy policy content from a given Uniform Resource Locator (URL). Furthermore, we will also demonstrate how the privacy policy is benchmarked using machine learning based on the EU GDPR aspects as criteria. The tool presents a risk-based, condensed and easy to understand summary of lengthy privacy policies in short period of time. We use user-friendly icons and colors to indicate the associated risk and draw user's attention. A screen cast of the tool is available on Youtube 3. The tool is tested to work on Macbook Pro OS X Yosemite 2.3 GHz Intel Core i7, and Microsoft Surface Pro 3 device with Windows 10 Pro OS, 8GB RAM, Intel Core i5-4300U CPU @ 1.90 GHz (Boost: 2.50 Ghz) and ADSL 2+ Internet connection. It requires a stable Internet connection to extract the privacy policy from target web services.

Limitations:. While this paper takes important steps towards building a stable tool that summarizes lengthy privacy policies in a short period of time, we also acknowledge that there are certain limitations, particularly: 1) PrivacyGuide works for privacy policies in English language only; 2) at its current phase, it works as stand alone tool, which in itself might have privacy gains, but it also presents some challenges with respect to model updating; 3) PrivacyGuide provides important insights about service provider's promises, however, as with any machine learning model, there are false positives and false negatives in the classification process. Thus, the tool should be taken as supportive but non-biding input for decisions. Future works:. We envision to enhance the prediction accuracy of PrivacyGuide by enlarging the privacy policy corpus and also experimenting with machine learning techniques that can better grasp the semantics of the privacy policy text, e.g., using word embeddings and deep learning techniques. Furthermore, we also plan to run usability and user adoption studies. Last but not least, we envisage to build a holistic information privacy enhacing framework composed of (1) PrivacyGuide; (2) default privacy preference setting prediction engine [7]; and (3) unintended information disclosure detection and quantification component [11].
